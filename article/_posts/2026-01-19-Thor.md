## Problem Setting and Motivation

The paper addresses unsupervised anomaly detection in medical imaging, where only *healthy* data are available during training. The goal is to detect and localize abnormalities at test time by identifying deviations from the learned healthy data distribution. Diffusion models are attractive for this task because they provide a principled generative framework capable of reconstructing images from noise. However, standard diffusion-based reconstruction tends to over-correct images, modifying not only pathological regions but also healthy anatomy, which leads to reduced specificity and noisy anomaly maps.

## Background: Diffusion Models for Anomaly Detection

Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) define a forward noising process and a learned reverse denoising process. The forward process gradually corrupts a clean image $$x_0$$ into Gaussian noise:


$$ q(x_t \mid x_0) = \mathcal{N}\left(x_t; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t} x_0, (1 - \bar{\alpha}_t)\mathbf{I}\right),
\quad \bar{\alpha}_t = \prod_{s=1}^{t} (1 - \beta_s) $$

which leads to 

$$x_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t} \, x_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} \, \epsilon_t$$

The reverse process is parameterized by a neural network $$\epsilon_\theta$$ trained to predict the injected noise:

$$
\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) \approx \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})
$$

which leads to

$$x_{t-1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} \left(x_t - \frac{1-\alpha_t}{\sqrt{1- \bar{\alpha}_t}}\epsilon_{\theta}\right) + \sigma_t \, \epsilon$$

For anomaly detection, a test image is diffused to an intermediate timestep $$t$$, then reconstructed back toward a healthy-looking image $$\hat{x}_0$$. Anomaly maps are typically computed as a residual:

$$
m(x_0,\hat{x}_0) = \lvert x_0 - \hat{x}_0 \rvert
$$

This approach implicitly assumes that the model modifies only abnormal regions, which in practice is often violated.

## Method Overview: Implicitly Guided Diffusion (THOR)

The paper proposes **THOR (Temporal Harmonization for Optimal Restoration)**, a diffusion-based anomaly detection method that introduces *implicit guidance* during the reverse diffusion process.
The key idea is that anomaly information emerges progressively during reverse diffusion and can be reused to constrain future denoising steps, without introducing an explicit external guidance network.

## Temporal Anomaly Estimation

At each reverse diffusion step $$t$$, the model produces an estimate of the clean image $$\hat{x}_0^{(t)}$$. A *temporal anomaly map* is defined as:

$$
m(x_0,\hat{x}_0^{(t)}) = \lvert x_0 - \hat{x}_0^{(t)} \rvert * S_{LPIPS}(x_0,\hat{x}_0^{(t)})
$$

Where $$S_{LPIPS} $$ is a Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS) metric.
These maps encode how strongly each pixel deviates from the model’s notion of healthy anatomy at a given diffusion scale. Early steps capture coarse structural deviations, while later steps emphasize finer details.

## Implicit Guidance via Harmonized Reverse Diffusion

Instead of sampling purely from the standard reverse transition:

$$
p_\theta(x_{t-1} \mid x_t) = \mathcal{N}\left(x_{t-1}; \mu_\theta(x_t, t), \Sigma_t \right),
$$

THOR modifies the mean update to re-inject information from the original image in regions that are likely healthy. Conceptually, its "harmonization" process can be written as:

$$
\tilde{x}_{t} = cd(m(x_0,\hat{x}_0^{(t)}))*\hat{x}_0^{(t)}+(1-cd(m(x_0,\hat{x}_0^{(t)})) )*x_0
$$

where $$cd$$ is a closing and dilation mophological operation.  This formulation biases the reconstruction toward the input image in healthy regions, while allowing the diffusion model to overwrite anomalous areas.

Importantly, this guidance is *implicit*: it does not require gradients, classifiers, or additional supervision.


## Experimental Evaluation

The method is evaluated on brain MRI lesion detection and pediatric wrist X-ray anomaly localization. Across datasets, THOR consistently improves localization accuracy compared to standard diffusion-based baselines. Qualitative results show better preservation of healthy anatomy and reduced spurious activations, particularly in structurally complex regions.
