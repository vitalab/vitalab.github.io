# EchoJEPA

EchoJEPA is a foundation model for echocardiography based on the V-JEPA2 architecture. 
It is trained on 18 million videos from 300K subjects.

The authors demonstrate superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods across multiple downstream tasks,
highlighting the potential of JEPA-based approaches for echocardiography.

![](/article/images/echojepa/training.png)

In addition to the largest model trained on 18 million videos (ViT-Giant, 1.1B params), 
authors release a smaller EchoJEPA-L models (ViT-Large, 300M) trained on 525K public videos from MIMIC-IV Echo. 

## Background

I-JEPA and V-JEPA models were developed and trained at the embedding level rather than the pixel level, such as VideoMAE models. 
This rewards semantically stable structures rather than surface statistics, 
and is an interesting avenue for Echocardiography, as most of the pixel-level information is noise.

Other models developed include PanEcho and VLMs such as EchoCLIP and EchoPrime.

## Method 

### Pre-training

The method is composed of three main components: the encoder $E_\theta$, predictor $P_\phi$ and the EMA Encoder $E_\overline{\theta}$.

The encoder provides the predictor with representations from visible tubelets in the masked input video. 
Then, with a learnable mask token $\Delta_y$, the predictor infers embeddings of masked regions.
The EMA encoder (with a stop-gradient $sg$) produces full embeddings and a L1 loss is calculated between predicted and target embeddings:

$$\mathcal{L} = ||P_\phi(\Delta_y, E_\theta(x)) - sg(E_\overline{\theta})||_1.$$

The target encoder weights are updated at each training step with the expected moving average.
This helps avoid model collapse.

### Downstream tasks
For downstream tasks, a multi-view attention probe is used to combine video embeddings with learnable view and a clip embeddings.
The tokens are processed through masked self-attention blocks then aggregated via a learnable cross-attention query 
into a single representation for downstream prediction

![](/article/images/echojepa/downstream.png)

## Evaluation

Authors also propose a unified evaluation methodology to compare all models on a level playing field. 
This includes a standardized attention probe to extend models' capabilities to clinical tasks.

## Results
The largest variant of the EchoJEPA model performs the best across view classification, LVEF and RVSP regression.
![](/article/images/echojepa/LVEF.png) 
![](/article/images/echojepa/view_classif.png)
![](/article/images/echojepa/RVSP.png)


When compared, with the same architecture and training data, JEPA performs much better than MAE, 
suggesting that it learns a more semantically informative embedding space.
![](/article/images/echojepa/comparison_to_MAE.png)

EchoJEPA is also more robust to depth attenuation artefacts and acoustic shadows synthetically added to random images.

However, across some experiments, EchoJEPA-L (smaller model, trained on less data) shows a significant performance decrease, 
performing worse than EchoPrime and PanEcho. 
This likely indicates that the number of training examples contained in only MIMIC-IV Echo is somewhat insufficient.

Attention maps show that, compared to pixel-level training in EchoMAE, EchoJEPA focuses on clinically relevant regions of images.

![](/article/images/echojepa/attentionmaps.png)

Moreover, a representation of the latent space shows that the embeddings learned with the JEPA architecture are far more separable.

![](/article/images/echojepa/latentspace-umap.png)

## Interpretation and conclusion

Interesting avenue, with speckle noise and artefacts mitigated in training by nature,
this method is an interesting avenue for foundation models and encoders for VLLM models.
